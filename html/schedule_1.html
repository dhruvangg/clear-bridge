Schedule 1: INFORMED CONSENT FORM FOR CELL COLLECTION AND BIOBANKING
(CYROGENIC STORAGE)

1. Introduction and Purpose

You are being asked to provide a blood sample for the specific purpose of collecting and storing your cells for your own potential future use in developing a personalized cell therapy. This process is called "biobanking" or "cell banking."

The cells we are collecting are called Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells (PBMCs), a type of white blood cell that can be used as a starting material to develop advanced therapies, such as genetically modified immune cell therapies (e.g., CAR-T cells).

This consent form will explain the process, the risks, and how your cells will be stored and potentially used. Your decision to participate is entirely voluntary. Please read this information carefully and ask any questions you have before you decide.

2. What Will Happen During the Cell Collection?

If you agree to participate:

  • • A healthcare professional will collect a blood sample from a vein in your arm.
  • • The amount of blood collected will be approximately 100 ml.
  • • This blood draw is similar to a standard lab test but takes a few minutes longer due to the larger volume.
  • • Your blood will be sent to a specialized laboratory where your PBMCs will be isolated, processed, tested for infectious diseases, and frozen in secure storage tanks for long-term preservation.
  • • Any other terms that are specified in the Clause 4 of the "Agreement" will apply to the collection process.
3. What Are the Potential Future Uses of My Banked Cells?

Your banked PBMCs are intended for your use only (autologous use). The primary purpose is to hold them in storage so they are available if you and your physician decide to pursue a cell therapy treatment in the future.

This future treatment could involve:

  • • Using your stored cells as the "starting material" to manufacture a personalized therapy product. The cells may be genetically modified or expanded in a laboratory to create a therapeutic dose.
  • • Any future use of your cells to manufacture a therapy would require a "separate, specific consent process". You will be fully informed about the specific therapy, its risks, benefits, and experimental nature at that time.

Note: While CellEntrust shall make reasonable efforts to store PBMCs under optimized conditions, it does not guarantee suitability of stored materials for any downstream applications, which remain subject to applicable laws and independent assessments.

Release of PBMCs may be precluded by regulations or orders relating to contamination, communicable infectious diseases, or other issues outside CellEntrust's reasonable control.

4. What Are the Risks of the Cell Collection?

The primary risks are those associated with a standard blood draw, though the larger volume may slightly increase these risks:

Pain/Discomfort

Brief sting or pinch from the needle.

Bleeding or Bruising (Hematoma)

May occur at the needle site.

Infection

A small risk at the needle site. Sterile technique is used to minimize this.

Lightheadedness, Dizziness, or Fainting

These can occur with a larger blood draw.

Fatigue

You may feel tired afterwards.

6. What Happens If the Storage Facility Has a Problem?

The storage facility uses alarm systems, backup power, and liquid nitrogen tanks designed for long-term integrity. While every precaution is taken, unforeseen events like equipment failure, natural disasters, or supplier issues could potentially compromise your sample.

Important: CellEntrust will not be liable for such unforeseen events that lead to the loss or damage of the sample despite following standard operating procedures.

7. Your Rights Regarding Your Banked Cells

You have the following rights, which you may exercise at any time by contacting CellEntrust at +852 9349 3588

Right to Withdraw:

You can change your mind and withdraw your consent for the storage of your cells. You must comply with the terms set out in the Clause 11 of the "Agreement".

Options Upon Withdrawal:

  1. 1. Discard – Have your cells "Discarded" in a medically safe and ethical process.
  2. 2. Quality Control and/or Research Donation – Donate your cells for approved research
  3. 3. Transfer – Request transfer of your cells to another facility. (Note: This may not be possible due to the frozen state and regulations. If it is, you would have to bear associated costs)
